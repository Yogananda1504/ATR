Genetic Sequencing: An Overview

**Definition and Process**

Genetic sequencing, also referred to as genomic or DNA sequencing, is the laboratory process of determining the complete or partial sequence of DNA from an organism or a sample. This involves extracting DNA—often from blood or tissue—preparing it for analysis, and then determining the order of nucleotides (the building blocks of DNA) to reveal an organism’s entire genetic makeup or targeted regions of interest (NCI Dictionary of Cancer Terms; [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_sequencing)).

**Technological Generations of Sequencing**

Genetic sequencing technologies have evolved significantly, typically described in three generations:

- **First Generation**: Sanger sequencing (sequencing by synthesis) and Maxam-Gilbert sequencing (sequencing by cleavage) were pioneering methods. Sanger sequencing remains notable for its accuracy and long read lengths, though it is less scalable for large genomes ([Illumina](https://www.illumina.com/techniques/sequencing/dna-sequencing.html)).
- **Next Generation Sequencing (NGS)**: This introduced higher throughput and the ability to sequence millions of fragments in parallel, significantly increasing speed and reducing cost. However, read lengths are generally shorter, making NGS particularly useful for resequencing known genomes ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27896715/)).
- **Third Generation/Real-Time Sequencing**: Emerging technologies in this category aim to further improve speed, reduce costs, and increase accuracy, often delivering real-time results and longer read lengths.

**Types of Sequencing Methods**

- **Whole-Genome Sequencing**: Determines the entire DNA sequence of an organism’s genome, providing the most comprehensive analysis.
- **Exome Sequencing**: Targets only the protein-coding regions (exons), which constitute about 1% of the genome but are most relevant for many genetic diseases.
- **Targeted Resequencing**: Focuses on specific genes or genomic regions of interest, making it cost-effective for particular questions or conditions.
- **Cell-Free DNA Sequencing**: Analyzes short DNA fragments circulating in the bloodstream, allowing non-invasive prenatal testing and cancer monitoring ([Illumina](https://www.illumina.com/techniques/sequencing/dna-sequencing.html)).

**Workflow of Genetic Sequencing**

A typical sequencing workflow includes:
1. DNA extraction from the sample
2. Library preparation (fragmenting DNA and adding adaptors)
3. Sequencing using the chosen technology
4. Data analysis (assembling and interpreting sequences)
5. Interpretation of results for research or clinical application

**Applications of Genetic Sequencing**

Genetic sequencing has revolutionized a wide range of fields:

- **Medical Research and Diagnostics**: Identifies genetic mutations and disease associations, enables accurate diagnoses, and guides targeted treatments ([NCI Dictionary of Cancer Terms](https://www.cancer.gov/publications/dictionaries/cancer-terms/def/genomic-sequencing)).
- **Oncology**: Analyzes tumor DNA for mutations and monitors disease progression.
- **Conservation Biology**: Assesses genetic diversity in endangered species for conservation strategies.
- **Forensic Science**: Assists in human identification and solving criminal cases.
- **Evolutionary Biology**: Helps reconstruct evolutionary relationships and histories among species.
- **Genotyping**: Examines genetic variation and its functional consequences ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_sequencing)).

**Trends and Accessibility**

Declining costs and improvements in sequencing technology are rapidly increasing the accessibility and throughput of genetic sequencing, enabling broader applications in both research and clinical settings ([PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27896715/)).
